Bottom Line

Cloudflare's cross-functional security and talent team is actively exploring hiring fraud solutions but expressed strong skepticism about legal liability and end-to-end vendor consolidation; Clarity's unified platform resonated with the talent side, while security leadership (Blake) pushed hard on legal risk and false positive handling -- follow-up hinges on internal alignment.

Executive Summary

A cross-functional Cloudflare team spanning CERT/incident response (Craig Strubhart), physical security (Eric Jackson, ex-FBI), talent acquisition (Ian Frost, Jack Machat, Jon Gulick), and security leadership (Blake) met with Clarity for a discovery call. Ian Frost organized the meeting after an initial call with Jack Machat, positioning it as due diligence to determine if Clarity aligns with any of their active work streams.

Jacob presented Clarity's end-to-end hiring fraud detection covering CV screening, deepfake detection, bio-comparison across interviews, IDV, and post-hire reverification. The team showed strong interest in the unified platform concept -- particularly around the gap between siloed tools that don't cross-reference across hiring stages. However, Blake (joining via phone) challenged aggressively on legal liability around false positives and the human-in-the-loop compliance model, creating a tense exchange. Craig asked thoughtful questions about data storage, privacy, hashing vs. video retention, and step-up verification. The call ended cordially with Ian saying they'd regroup internally.

Key Insights

Multi-Vendor Landscape

Cloudflare already has 3-4 vendors doing pieces of this (resume screening, Hyper for something else). Blake's core concern: fragmentation is bad, but so is trusting one vendor to do everything. They want customizability.

Internal Resume Screening

They're already building internal resume screening capabilities. Craig mentioned overlap with what they're "already exploring." Clarity needs to show value beyond what they can build themselves.

Legal Liability is a Hot Button

Blake pushed hard: if Clarity's human-in-the-loop makes the call, who bears legal responsibility for a false positive? Jacob's answer ("we haven't had false negatives") triggered Blake's strongest pushback -- seen as a sign of insufficient scale.

Privacy-Conscious

Craig asked detailed questions about hashing vs. storing video/voice data, S3 bucket ownership options, and retention policies. Enterprise privacy review will be a gate.

Product Fit Analysis

1

End-to-End Unified Platform

Jacob's strongest pitch point: siloed tools don't cross-reference across hiring stages. If IDV is standalone, you don't know if the person who verified is the same person who interviewed. This argument resonated with Ian and Craig.

2

Post-Hire Step-Up Verification

Craig showed genuine interest in step-up reverification for password resets and sensitive operations. The ability to trigger face/voice analysis on demand -- and join sensitive finance calls to prevent deepfake fraud -- was a differentiator.

3

Flexible Data Storage

Option for Cloudflare to host data in their own S3 bucket, with hash-only biometric storage, addresses Craig's and Eric's privacy/ownership concerns directly.

4

Greenhouse Integration

Jacob demonstrated Greenhouse integration. Need to confirm Cloudflare's ATS -- not explicitly stated but talent team seemed familiar with the workflow.

Risk and Opportunity Assessment

Opportunity Signals

  • Large enterprise with active insider threat concerns and multiple teams engaged
  • Cross-functional attendance (security + talent + physical security) shows organizational buy-in
  • Craig genuinely interested in step-up verification and ad-hoc deepfake bot for sensitive calls
  • Ian organized the call proactively after initial meeting with Jack -- internal champion
  • They already have 3-4 fragmented vendors -- consolidation argument has legs
  • Prior relationship through Jack Machat (R&D recruiting)

Risk Factors

  • Blake is a strong skeptic with authority -- challenged legal liability, product maturity, and customer base size
  • Blake's parting comment ("you're gonna get sued") signals fundamental distrust of the compliance model
  • They're building internal resume screening -- may not want to replace with external tool
  • Blake values multi-vendor customizability over single-platform convenience
  • No clear timeline or budget commitment discussed
  • Jacob's "we haven't had false negatives" response was poorly received -- needs better messaging

Coaching Notes

Legal Liability Response

Blake's challenge was legitimate and predictable. "We haven't had false negatives" sounds like inexperience, not confidence. Better: "We provide explainable evidence and recommendations. The hiring decision always remains yours. Our compliance framework is designed for exactly this -- here's our legal documentation." Have legal docs ready to share.

Handling Hostile Skeptics

Blake was testing, not attacking. "Let's move on" came across as dismissive. Better to acknowledge the concern directly: "That's a fair challenge. Let me walk you through exactly how our liability framework works." Engage, don't deflect.

False Positive Framing

Never say "we haven't had X." Enterprise security leaders hear "you don't have enough data." Instead: "Our false positive rate is X%. When flags occur, here's our explainability process..." Even if the rate is near-zero, frame it as a measured metric, not an absence.

Multi-Vendor Argument

Blake's customizability point is valid. Don't dismiss it. Acknowledge: "You're right that no single tool catches everything. Our value isn't replacing all your tools -- it's connecting the identity thread across stages that no point solution can." Meet them where they are.
